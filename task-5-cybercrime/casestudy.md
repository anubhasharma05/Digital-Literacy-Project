Case Study: UPI / Online Payment Fraud

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) fraud is a rapidly growing form of cybercrime in India, where attackers manipulate users into authorizing transactions through popular payment apps such as Google Pay, PhonePe, and Paytm. This type of fraud exploits a lack of awareness about how UPI transactions actually work.

Consider a realistic scenario involving a college student named radha. She posts an advertisement online to sell her old laptop. Soon after, she receives a call from a potential buyer who seems genuine and agrees to pay immediately. The scammer claims he will transfer the money via UPI and sends her a “collect request.” Since radha expects to receive money, she approves the request without carefully reading the notification. Instead of receiving money, ₹15,000 is deducted from her account.

In a typical UPI fraud, the process unfolds step-by-step. First, the scammer identifies a target through online marketplaces, social media, or random calls. Next, they build trust by pretending to be a buyer, bank official, or service provider. Then, they create urgency or confusion, pressuring the victim to act quickly. After that, they send a payment request or QR code and mislead the victim into believing it is for receiving money. Finally, once the victim approves the request or enters their UPI PIN, the money is instantly transferred to the scammer’s account.

Common targets include students, senior citizens, and first-time digital payment users who may not fully understand the difference between “sending” and “receiving” money on UPI platforms. People in urgent situations are especially vulnerable.

The consequences of such fraud are serious. Victims may suffer immediate financial loss, emotional distress, and reduced trust in digital systems. Recovery of money is often difficult due to the speed of transactions. Additionally, victims may unknowingly expose personal or banking information, increasing the risk of further fraud.

This case highlights the importance of digital awareness—users must remember that receiving money does not require entering a PIN or approving a request.
